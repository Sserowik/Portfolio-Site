ThreadCraft

The concept for threadCraft was simple;

Create a place for people of all bodies to shop for high-end, classy, nerdy gear.

The brand guidelines make the mission clear-

“Our clothing line strives to give you the nerdcore choices you need to forge your fit. Whether its something professional to wear to your next board meeting or something more casual for your next board game night, we can help you gear up. With our gender-free sizing, disability aware cuts, and a wide range of measurements available, you will be sure to find the perfect armor set for your next adventure.”

I wanted to create a place for people to celebrate themselves. No matter what you're into or what your body may look like, threadCraft would be a place where you could shop. I often have found that “nicer” clothes never fit me quite right, and they definitely fail to express who I, as a professional and an artist, truly am. I have never found a place that both provides me the comfortable, clean fit I desire mixed with patterns and colors that emphasize my personality. I set out to create threadCraft to fix that.

So, Why Nerdcore?

I went in this direction for 2 reasons-

Nerdcore is one of the most flexible, inviting genres I grew up with. From middle aged rappers creating flows about the Atari 2600 to young kids landing hooks about Disney Princesses, it welcomes all types.
The overarching style is one that not many highend clothing products lean into while remaining accesible. You can easily find expensive and fun Nerdcore inspired clothing, but- more often than not- its not something made for everyBODY.

I think due to the flowing nature of the genre and the flexibility of the concepts, creators don't want to try and put certain elements in stone- color, fonts, language, etc.- unless they can massmarket the final product. I believe, however, that there is a way to utilize the concepts present in the genre, remaining open to changes in the specifics as time passes, while still being accessible to all. Colors and fonts we use today may well need an update tomorrow, just stay true to the main principles.

User Stories

Both the sizing available and styling would be something users were struggling to find in physical locations, whether it was due to time constraints or availability. More and more people are shopping on their down time- the commute to work, lunch breaks, lying in bed- so the app would need to accommodate users who could not make it to a physical location. In addition, a lot of upcoming professionals are also finding it harder and harder to afford the nicer clothes they desire, so it would be key to offer a sales selection. These constraints generated 3 user stories for me to lean into during this initial design process-

As a new customer, I want to access the inventory without having to register, so that I can make sure this store has what I'm looking for before having to create an account.
As a customer, I want to be able to see when stock is on sale, so that I can save money.
As a customer, I want to have access to advanced filtering options, so that I don't have to browse through a lot of products to find what I'm looking for.

User Testing

I used the low-fidelity screens to conduct some initial rounds of testing. I wanted to know what users would be looking for, what was I missing. If I was going to promise a unique experience in product and sizing, what would users actually want to see?
